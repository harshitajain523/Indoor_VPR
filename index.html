Auditing Indoor Visual Place Recognition

A dataset landscape survey, and an empirical study of ground-truth protocols and evaluation artifacts

Overview

Visual place recognition (VPR) asks a simple question: given a photograph, which images in a reference database were taken at the same place? This project studies VPR indoors, where the problem is harder and the evaluation is less standardised than outdoors. It has two parts.

  1. A dataset landscape survey. A structured review of about twenty-five VPR datasets released between 2007 and 2025, with a taxonomy, a sensor-modality matrix, an evaluation-protocol table, and a catalogue of gaps that are specific to indoor VPR.
  2. An empirical audit. A controlled benchmarking of two retrieval methods, SegVLAD (segment-based) and MegaLoc (a single global descriptor), across four indoor datasets. The emphasis is not on ranking the methods but on making the numbers interpretable: how sensitive a recall value is to the choice of ground-truth threshold, how it compares to random chance, and how dataset artifacts such as privacy anonymisation affect it.

The two parts reinforce each other. The survey identifies that indoor VPR has no unified evaluation protocol and that anonymisation is rarely documented. The audit then measures exactly those effects on real datasets.

Background: how VPR is measured, and why indoors is different

The retrieval task. Each image is converted into a descriptor (a vector). For a query, the database images are ranked by descriptor similarity. The query counts as correct at Recall@N if at least one of the top N ranked images is a true same-place image, called a positive. Which database images count as positives is fixed by a ground truth: often a distance threshold in the world (for example, within r metres of the query), sometimes a window of neighbouring frame indices.

Why the ground truth matters so much. Recall depends entirely on how positives are defined. Widen the radius and more database images qualify, so recall rises, but so does the score a random ranker would achieve. This is why we report a shuffled-retriever floor next to every recall.

Indoor VPR is structurally different from outdoor VPR. The survey summarises five differences:

  • No GPS, so ground truth is a 6-DoF camera pose or a room label rather than a satellite coordinate.
  • Datasets are one to two orders of magnitude smaller than outdoor benchmarks.
  • The sources of change are furniture, lighting, clutter, and doors, not weather and season.
  • Perceptual aliasing. Modern architecture produces identical-looking hallways, floors, and stairwells, so geometric verification frequently fails.
  • Privacy. High-traffic indoor spaces require anonymising the people in the images, which introduces artifacts into the evaluation data itself.

Part 1: A VPR dataset landscape survey

The survey covers roughly twenty-five datasets from 2007 to 2025 and organises them into a master taxonomy: outdoor (large-scale and long-term, multi-modal and range-based, urban retrieval, compact baselines), indoor (static and relocalization, repeat-visit and change-aware, text-assisted and repetitive-structure), pedestrian-centric, and adjacent localization benchmarks. It also provides a sensor-modality matrix that tracks RGB, depth, thermal, LiDAR, radar, GPS or IMU, and, as an emerging modality, scene text.

The most important output is a catalogue of six gaps that this benchmarking work then acts on:

  1. No unified indoor benchmark protocol. Outdoor VPR has converged on Recall@N against shared splits, whereas indoor work uses pose error, robustness splits, and room-label accuracy independently. A cross-dataset benchmarking pass under one protocol is a concrete open contribution.
  2. Indoor long-term data scarcity. Few indoor datasets model repeated visits over time.
  3. Indoor scale deficit. The largest indoor set surveyed has about 89,550 images, below the outdoor Pittsburgh 250k and far below million-image benchmarks.
  4. Repetitive structure and perceptual aliasing. There is no standard benchmark or metric for measuring how robust a method is to aliasing.
  5. Scene text as a modality. Room numbers and signs are becoming a standard cue for disambiguating repetitive structures, yet most modality surveys omit them.
  6. Privacy and anonymisation. Anonymisation is rarely documented. NYC-Indoor-VPR masks pedestrians with white pixels; most other datasets do not state their protocol at all.

Part 2: Methods and setup

Two retrieval methods are compared.

  • SegVLAD (Garg et al., ECCV 2024) retrieves image segments rather than whole-image descriptors. Segment Anything (SAM) decomposes each image into segments, DINOv2 features each segment, segments are aggregated into VLAD descriptors with neighbour aggregation, and a similarity-weighted bin count over retrieved segments produces the final image ranking. The runs here use the default configuration exp0_global_SegLoc_VLAD_PCA_o3 (neighbour-aggregation order 3 with PCA) and the pretrained backbone (PreT).
  • MegaLoc (Berton and Masone, 2025) produces a single global descriptor per image (a DINOv2 ViT-B backbone with SALAD aggregation, 8448 dimensions, about 228 million parameters), at a stated inference resolution of 322 by 322.

Hardware. Full SAM and DINO extraction ran on the Phoenix server (an RTX A4000). A local RTX 4050 with 6 GB was used for MegaLoc inference and for validation, since MegaLoc needs no segmentation step and fits comfortably in 6 GB.

Three observations follow.

  • The segment advantage that SegVLAD shows on 17-Places does not carry over to repetitive corridors. At Recall@1 it is essentially tied with the appearance-only method SALAD (about 61.6).
  • The gap between Recall@1 and Recall@5 is about 20 points on maze, against about 3 points on 17-Places. The correct place is usually present in the top five but loses at rank 1. The figure below makes this visible.
  • Only the pretrained backbone was run. A finetuned backbone would likely improve the numbers, and scene-text re-ranking (the TextInPlace contribution) adds about 14 points on its own.

The failure mode is cross-floor confusion. The typical rank-1 miss is a same-looking corridor or pillar on the wrong floor, for example the fifth floor confused with the second. A note on reading the montages in the report: the maze ground truth is position-only and orientation-agnostic, so an image counted as a valid positive can face a different direction from the query and therefore look quite different. That is a property of the 10 m radius protocol, not a labelling error. The full montages of rank-1 hits and misses are in the Benchmark Report (PDF).

One caution carried over from the audit: a between-method gap is only meaningful when both methods used the same ground-truth radius. The MegaLoc and SegVLAD maze numbers here are both at the 10 m protocol; a difference in radius alone can manufacture a gap of this size.

Ground-truth protocols are load-bearing

Two independent observations show that a single recall number is not self-interpreting indoors.

  • Radius sensitivity. The SegVLAD paper itself reports that Recall@1 on 17-Places swings from about 67% to about 95% depending only on the frame-radius chosen for positives, with SegVLAD and AnyLoc tracking each other as the radius changes. The choice of threshold, not just the method, moves the headline number.
  • Chance depends on database size. The teaser figure shows that at a fixed 25 m radius, a random ranker scores about 17% Recall@1 on the 689-image Baidu subset but only about 5% on the 5,207-image build. A wide radius on a small database inflates every method's number relative to chance.

The response is a fixed practice, described below, of reading recall against a chance floor and taking thresholds from the benchmark's stated protocol rather than choosing them to produce a desired result.

Anonymisation artifacts in the evaluation data

The Maze-with-Text benchmark anonymises people by replacing them with baked-in pure-white silhouettes. A connected-component detector run over all 9,845 images finds the artifact in about 23% of both reference and query images (23.3% and 22.8% at the bimodal-valley threshold). Because the silhouettes sit on both sides of retrieval, they are a property of the corpus rather than of any one method.

This connects directly to the survey's privacy gap. NYC-Indoor-VPR also uses white-pixel masking, while the NAVER LABS localisation datasets instead blur the upper body of each person. Every crowded-indoor dataset carries some anonymisation artifact; the form differs, and pure-white silhouettes are likely to affect a segmentation-based method differently from a global descriptor, since the segmenter is handed a large uninformative blob.

Building a clean subset is harder than a single threshold. A cut on white-blob size cannot guarantee a fully clean subset, because small distant silhouettes overlap genuinely clean bright windows on the same size axis. A shape-aware and context-aware detector, followed by a visual audit of the survivors, is required to assert that no artifact remains. Removing masked references also cascades into the queries, since a query with no clean positive within the radius can no longer be scored, and it produces a sparser database, so a fair comparison needs a size-matched random-drop control.

In progress: cross-floor ground truth on InLoc

MegaLoc's author states that discarding the vertical coordinate and computing positives from horizontal distance alone is safe indoors, because it is rare to credit a retrieval that shares a query's horizontal position but sits on a different floor. Every common indoor benchmark (Baidu, 17-Places, single-building maze retrieval) is effectively single-storey, so the claim has never been testable. InLoc is multi-storey, which makes it the setting where the claim becomes falsifiable.

InLoc structure. The database has 9,972 cutouts from WUSTL RGB-D scans across five floors (CSE3, CSE4, CSE5, DUC1, DUC2), with verified per-floor cutout counts of 252, 2700, 2340, 1800, and 2880. The 356 iPhone 7 query photographs are taken on DUC1 and DUC2 only, so the other three floors act as pure confusers. Query ground-truth poses are withheld to an evaluation server, while database cutout poses are recoverable from the dataset's alignment files.

The measurement. In a multi-storey building, how often does a horizontal-distance ground truth credit a retrieval that is physically on a different floor? Because the query poses are withheld, the planned design holds out a spatially separated set of database scans as pseudo-queries, which gives exact poses on both sides. A verification pass then separates genuine cross-floor visibility (an atrium view) from architectural doppelgangers (an identical corridor one floor up).

A candidate testbed. The NAVER LABS localisation datasets (a Hyundai department store over three floors, a Gangnam metro station over two) provide dense 6-DoF ground truth for training and validation queries, so query poses are available. They are a strong alternative to InLoc for this study, with the caveat that they apply upper-body blur and require registration to download.

Infrastructure and reproducibility

A storage incident shaped the current status. During the full runs the Phoenix /scratch2 volume failed with I/O errors mid-process. Extracted features, PCA models, and intermediate results were lost. Code, fixes, model checkpoints, the maze numbers, and the montages survived on the laptop. The rough recompute cost is about 4 hours for Baidu, 13 hours for maze, and 13 hours for InLoc, and the re-runs are blocked on roughly 350 GB of scratch space.

Repository hardening completed along the way: removal of a headless-unsafe tkinter import, atomic HDF5 writes (write to a temporary file, then rename), resumable and skippable pipeline stages, an image-extension filter that fixed an InLoc extraction crash, and a working InLoc mirror download.

Reproducibility practices applied throughout:

  • Every recall is reported next to a shuffled-retriever floor (10 seeds, mean).
  • Ground-truth parameters are taken from each benchmark's stated protocol, never tuned to reach a target number.
  • Descriptor order is checked against filename order on every extraction, to catch the silent bug where the two diverge.
